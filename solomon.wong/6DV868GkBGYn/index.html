Event-driven architecture, visually explained

Instead of services calling each other and waiting for answers, services announce facts — and anyone who cares reacts in their own time. That one inversion changes how systems couple, scale and fail.

01 — The idea

What event-driven architecture is

In a request/response system, the caller knows exactly who it is talking to and blocks until it gets an answer: “charge this card, and tell me whether it worked.” In an event-driven system, a service publishes an event — an immutable record that something has already happened — to a broker, then carries on. It neither knows nor cares who is listening.

Three properties make an event an event, rather than a disguised remote call:

  • It is a fact, named in the past tense. OrderPlaced, not PlaceOrder. A fact cannot be rejected by a consumer, only reacted to.
  • It is immutable. Once published it is history; corrections are new events, not edits.
  • It carries no addressee. The producer names the event, not the recipients — that is what keeps the coupling loose.

02 — The big picture

Producers, broker, consumers

Every event-driven system reduces to the same three roles. Producers publish facts and move on. The broker stores them durably, in order, grouped into topics. Consumers subscribe to the topics they care about and process events at their own pace — a slow consumer never slows a producer down.

03 — Judgement

When to use it — and when not to

Event-driven architecture is a trade, not an upgrade. You give up simplicity and immediate answers; you gain decoupling, elasticity and history. The trade pays off in some situations and clearly does not in others.

Good fit

One fact, many reactions

An order placed today triggers email, invoicing, fraud scoring and analytics. With events, each reaction is an independent consumer; nobody maintains a growing list of calls in the checkout code.

Good fit

Spiky or bursty load

The broker is a shock absorber. A flash-sale burst queues up as events and consumers drain the backlog at their sustainable rate, instead of timing out under synchronous fan-out.

Good fit

Team autonomy

Teams integrate through event contracts rather than each other's release schedules. New capabilities ship as new consumers, without cross-team lockstep deployments.

Good fit

Audit and replay

A durable event log is a first-class history: rebuild a corrupted read model, backfill a new consumer, or answer “what exactly happened at 09:14?” by replaying the record.

Poor fit

The caller needs the answer now

A card authorisation must return approve/decline in the same interaction. Forcing a request through an event round-trip adds latency and complexity for no benefit.

Poor fit

Invariants that span services

“Usernames are globally unique” is painful to enforce over eventually consistent events. If several services must agree before an action commits, synchronous coordination is more honest.

Poor fit

A small system, one team

A broker, schema registry, consumer groups, dead-letter queues and replay tooling are real operational weight. A modest CRUD application does not earn it back.

Poor fit

Strict global ordering

Brokers order events within a partition, not across a whole system. If your workflow needs one true global sequence across many entities, events will fight you.

Rule of thumb: use a request when the caller cannot proceed without the answer; publish an event when the world merely needs to find out. Most real systems are a deliberate mix of both.
